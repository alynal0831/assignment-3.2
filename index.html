Fun Facts About Corgis

Corgis were originally bred as herding dogs in Wales, where their unique short stature proved surprisingly advantageous. Their low-to-the-ground build allowed them to nip at the heels of cattle and duck beneath their powerful kicks. Despite their famously stubby legs, these dogs are remarkably fast, intensely smart, and absolutely brimming with energy! There are actually two distinct breeds: the Pembroke Welsh Corgi and the Cardigan Welsh Corgi. While both share the same spirited nature, the Pembroke is more popular and often recognized by its docked (or naturally bobbed) tail, whereas the Cardigan has a long, bushy tail.

There are two main types of the breed: the Pembroke Welsh Corgi and the Cardigan Welsh Corgi. While they share a common Welsh heritage and the breed’s characteristic short stature, they are considered entirely separate breeds, with the most immediate difference being the tail. The Pembroke usually has a naturally bobbed or docked tail, which was historically done to signify a working dog and avoid a tax on pet dogs. In contrast, the Cardigan’s tail is long and fox-like, a key distinguishing feature often carried low to the ground. Cardigans are also typically a bit larger and heavier, possessing a slightly more rounded ear shape, while the Pembroke's ears tend to be more pointed.

Queen Elizabeth II famously owned more than 30 Pembroke Welsh Corgis throughout her life, creating an association that indelibly linked the charming, short-legged dogs to the British monarchy and helped make the breed world-famous. Her lifelong love affair with the breed began when she received her first Corgi, Susan, as an 18th birthday gift in 1944. All of the Queen's subsequent Corgis—and even her mixed-breed Dorgis (Corgi-Dachshund crosses)—were descended from this beloved matriarch. These royal companions were more than just pets; they were constant companions who often accompanied the Queen on her travels and even made humorous cameos, such as in the James Bond sketch for the 2012 London Olympics opening ceremony.

Corgis are widely known for their adorable and unique posture called the "sploot," which is a canine resting position where they lie completely flat on their bellies with their rear legs stretched straight out behind them. This distinctive pose, sometimes referred to as a "frog-leg" position, is particularly common among short-legged breeds like Corgis because their low-slung, flexible bodies make it easy and comfortable. Owners often notice them "splooting" when they are relaxed or trying to cool down, as spreading their limbs maximizes the amount of body surface area that can make contact with a cool floor.

Different Breeds

The two Corgi types, while both distinctly Welsh, maintain several differences, both physical and historical. The Pembroke Welsh Corgi is the more common type globally, largely due to its association with royalty. This breed is renowned for being particularly playful, affectionate, and fiercely loyal, often possessing a slightly more mischievous personality. Physically, it is known for its docked or naturally bobbed tail and its slightly fox-like head. The Cardigan Welsh Corgi, in contrast, is the older of the two breeds and has a slightly larger and heavier frame, with a more robust build and a deeper chest. The easiest way to spot a Cardigan is by its defining characteristic: a long, full, fox-like tail that acts as a natural rudder when they run and is carried low to the ground. Cardigans are generally considered a bit more mellow and steady than their Pembroke cousins.

History

Beards have a rich and varied history that spans across different cultures and eras. In ancient times, beards were often seen as a symbol of wisdom, strength, and social status. For instance, in ancient Egypt, pharaohs wore false beards as a sign of their divine authority. Similarly, in ancient Greece, beards were associated with masculinity and knowledge, with philosophers like Socrates and Plato famously sporting them.

Throughout the Middle Ages, beards continued to hold cultural significance. Knights and noblemen often grew beards as a sign of their bravery and honor. During the Renaissance, beards fell out of fashion in Europe, but they made a comeback in the 19th century with the Victorian era, where full beards became a symbol of the rugged, industrious man. Today, beards are a popular fashion statement and a form of self-expression for many men around the world.
